# Product

## Register

product

## Users

People who already know they should travel more. They have saved Airbnb wishlists, screenshotted Instagram destinations, and a bank account that says they could. What they don't have is the activation energy to push through the planning spiral: eight open tabs, cross-referencing flights and hotels and things to do, decision fatigue that ends with the laptop closed and the trip still unbooked. They are not first-time travelers. They are stuck travelers. Walter exists to get them off the couch.

## Product Purpose

Walter takes a person from "I should go somewhere" to "I'm booked" in one session. The product collapses a normally multi-day planning project (flights in one tab, hotels in another, activities in a third, a spreadsheet to compare costs) into one cart. The user answers a few questions, the AI assembles a complete trip with real, bookable flights, stays, and things to do, and the user makes one click instead of forty.

Success is a real booking: a confirmed itinerary in the user's email, attributed to Walter via affiliate links.

The product is named after Walter Mitty, who in the 2013 film breaks through a grey, null life and discovers that the world is enormous and he was made to be in it. Walter the product carries the same conviction: the user already knows they want to go, and the only thing standing in the way is friction.

## Brand Personality

Confident. Cinematic. Alive.

Walter sounds like a filmmaker, not a friend. He has been to the places. He has seen the light at 6am over Reykjavík and the empty third floor of the museum in Lisbon. He doesn't recommend trips, he prescribes them. He believes the world is wasted on people who stay home.

The voice is warm but not coddling. It assumes competence. Short sentences, earned superlatives. It never hypes, because the places themselves are enough.

## Anti-references

- **Kayak, Expedia, Booking.com.** Transactional grey portals. Every screen looks like a 1099. Walter is not a database with a UI.
- **Pinterest-pretty travel listicles** ("10 stunning beaches you have to see"). Saccharine, decontextualized, screensaver bait. Walter does not list, it commits.
- **Generic AI startup landings.** Gradient hero, three-icon feature row, "powered by AI" bragging. Walter does not announce that it is intelligent.

## Positive references

- **Patagonia.** Wild, sincere, indifferent to fashion. Brand as worldview.
- **Airbnb 2014–2017.** The "Belong Anywhere" era. Real photography, real humans, warm humanism, earned cosmopolitanism, before the corporate veneer.
- **The Secret Life of Walter Mitty (2013).** Source mythology. The Iceland and Greenland sequences.

## Design Principles

1. **Show the place, not the product.** Real photography always beats decoration. If a screen has space for an image, it is an image of where the user is going, not an illustration about going.
2. **Resist grey.** The default state of every other booking tool is institutional and lifeless. Walter's restraint is in service of warmth, not corporate neutrality. Tinted blacks, considered light, never null.
3. **The cart is the proof.** Walter is judged on the moment a real flight, a real hotel, and a real Tuesday dinner reservation sit next to each other, ready to book. Every page should drive toward that moment.
4. **No hype, no listicles.** Walter never says "amazing," "stunning," or "you have to see." He names the thing and trusts the user.
5. **Confidence in motion.** Animation is sparing and exponential. Nothing bounces. Nothing announces itself. Things move like things in a film: with intent.
